# EXP_003

## Goal
To test the **noisy channel hypothesis** formulated in [EXP_002](experiments/EXP_002.md). We reduced the input channels from 14 down to **4 primary respiratory/oximetry channels** (`SpO2`, `Flow`, `ribcage`, `abdo`) to evaluate whether removing uninformative/noisy channels improves model feature learning and apnea detection performance.


## Setup
- **Dataset & Patient Split**: Same patient-level 80/20 split as [EXP_002](experiments/EXP_002.md) (Train: 20 patients `ucddb002`–`ucddb023`; Test: 5 patients `ucddb024`–`ucddb028`).
- **Channels Used**: **4 channels** (`SpO2`, `Flow`, `ribcage`, `abdo`) — reduced from 14 channels in EXP_002.
- **Signal Processing & Normalization**: Resampled to 10 Hz, 30-second windows (300 samples/win), per-patient z-score normalization on normal windows clipped to $[-5.0, +5.0]$.
- **Training Hyperparameters**:
  - Model: `ApneaCNN` (4 input channels)
  - Optimizer: Adam (lr = $1\times 10^{-3}$)
  - Loss Function: `BCEWithLogitsLoss` with positive class weight `pos_weight = 10.0` (capped at 10.0x vs 5.0x in EXP_002).
  - Batch Size: 64 | Epochs: 20

## Observations 

- **Precision Trend Remains Constrained**: Precision remains nearly identical to [EXP_002](experiments/EXP_002.md) (peaking around **17.7%** across threshold sweeps, and **14.4%** at $t=0.50$ vs $13.9\%$ in EXP_002).
  - *Why*: Positive predictive value remains bottlenecked by false alarms generated from normal respiratory variations and class imbalance in unstratified patient data.
- **Sensitivity Degradation is Less Aggressive**: Sensitivity does not collapse as rapidly as in [EXP_002](experiments/EXP_002.md) across intermediate thresholds (e.g., at $t=0.30$, sensitivity is **71.8%** vs $40.8\%$ in EXP_002; at $t=0.50$, sensitivity is **23.4%** vs $12.6\%$).
  - *Why*: Probably because Stripping 10 non-essential channels removed irrelevant signal noise and allowed the CNN to focus directly on key respiratory effort and airflow patterns.
- **True Positives (TP) Increased Significantly**: True Positives nearly doubled from **91** in EXP_002 to **169** at default threshold $t=0.50$.
  - *Why*: Direct access to uncorrupted respiratory signals (`Flow`, `ribcage`, `abdo`, `SpO2`) enabled the model to capture almost twice as many true apnea events.
- **Macro F1 Score Follows Similar Ceiling**: Macro F1 score peaks around **0.465** at $t=0.50$, comparable to EXP_002's peak of **0.478**.
  - *Why*: Although True Positives increased, False Positives also rose (from 564 to 1001), causing overall accuracy to drop from $70.7\%$ to $61.9\%$, balancing out the F1 calculation.


## Hypothesis

1. **Inclusion of Near-Normal Patients in Training**: Patients with negligible apnea rates (e.g., `ucddb008`, `ucddb014`, `ucddb018`, `ucddb019` all $<1\%$ apnea) flood the training set with normal breathing windows, corrupting class gradients and forcing the model to learn noise as normal respiration.
2. **High Inter-Subject Physiological Variability**: Substantial baseline differences across individual patients remain an unaddressed obstacle without patient-stratified data splitting.


## Interpretation and Conclusion

- **Summary**: Filtering the input signals down to 4 core respiratory channels successfully helped the model surface **nearly double the true apnea events** ($\text{TP} = 169$ vs $91$, sensitivity $23.4\%$ vs $12.6\%$). However, the overall Macro F1 score remained constrained (**0.4652** vs **0.4779**) due to an accompanying increase in false positives.
- **Why Performance Remained Capped**: Removing noisy channels addressed input signal quality, but did not resolve dataset-level distribution distortion caused by near-normal patients ($< 1\%$ apnea) dominating training iterations.

## Next
- **Primary Hypothesis for Next Experiment**: Stratify the training/testing dataset by filtering out patients with $< 5\%$ apnea to evaluate whether training on clean, event-rich patient data eliminates false positive noise and elevates Macro F1.
