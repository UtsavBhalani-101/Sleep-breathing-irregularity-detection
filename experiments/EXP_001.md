# EXP_001

## Goal
To evaluate a baseline 5-class sleep stage classification model (W, N1, N2, N3, REM) on the PhysioNet Sleep-EDF (cassette) dataset using 10-fold Leave-One-Patient-Out (LOPO) cross-validation.


## Setup
- **Dataset**: PhysioNet Sleep-EDF Database (Cassette subset).
- **Subjects**: 10 patients (`SC400`, `SC401`, `SC402`, `SC403`, `SC404`, `SC405`, `SC406`, `SC407`, `SC408`, `SC409`).
- **Input Dimensions**: 30-second EEG/EOG/EMG signal windows ($5 \text{ channels} \times 3,000 \text{ samples}$ at 100 Hz sampling frequency).
- **Target Classes (AASM Standard - 5 Classes)**:
  - `0`: Wake (W)
  - `1`: N1 (Non-REM Stage 1 / Light Sleep)
  - `2`: N2 (Non-REM Stage 2 / Stable Sleep)
  - `3`: N3 (Non-REM Stage 3 / Deep Sleep)
  - `4`: REM (Rapid Eye Movement Sleep)
- **Validation Scheme**: 10-fold Leave-One-Patient-Out (LOPO) Cross-Validation.
  - In each fold, 9 patients are used for training (~24,000–25,000 epochs) and 1 patient is held out for testing (~2,500–2,800 epochs).
- **Training Parameters**:
  - Epochs per fold: 30
  - Loss function: Cross-Entropy Loss

## Observations
- **Dominance of Wake Stage (W)**: The Wake stage achieves a strong average F1 score of **0.871** across folds. This high performance stems from the high proportion of Wake epochs (~70–75% of total recording time) in the dataset.
- **Complete Failure on Transition Stage N1**: N1 stage performance is near zero across almost all patients (mean F1 = **0.077**). N1 comprises only ~2% of training data and shares morphological characteristics with Wake and N2, causing severe misclassification.
- **Extreme Inter-Subject Variability**: Test accuracy varies wildly across patients, ranging from **39.21%** (SC400) to **77.38%** (SC403). For patient SC400, Wake recall dropped to 46% due to high confusion with N3 and N2.
- **Sub-optimal Performance on N3 and REM**: Deep sleep (N3) and REM sleep yield low mean F1 scores of **0.115** and **0.190** respectively, frequently being misclassified as N2 or Wake.


## Hypothesis
1. **Severe Class Imbalance Distorts Loss Gradients**: Wake epochs constitute ~75% of all samples, while N1 makes up only ~2%. Unweighted Cross-Entropy loss heavily penalizes errors on Wake while allowing minority classes (N1, N3, REM) to be misclassified with minimal loss penalty.
2. **High Inter-Subject Electroencephalographic Shift**: EEG amplitude and frequency signatures vary significantly between individuals. Without per-patient adaptive normalization or domain alignment, models trained on 9 subjects fail to generalize to the baseline voltage distributions of held-out patients.
3. **Absence of Multi-Epoch Temporal Dynamics**: Human sleep staging relies on temporal continuity (e.g., transitions following ~90-minute sleep cycles). Scoring 30-second windows independently prevents the network from learning sequential transition rules (such as N1 → N2 → N3).
4. **Lack of Specialized Frequency Domain Features**: Raw time-series signals without explicit spectral feature extraction (e.g., Delta power for N3, Theta power for N1/REM, Alpha power for Wake, Sleep Spindle filters for N2) force the 1D CNN to discover complex frequency representations from limited data.


## Interpretation and Conclusion
- **Conclusion**: The baseline LOPO sleep staging model achieves an overall mean accuracy of **64.91%**, but performance is heavily skewed toward predicting the Wake class. The model fails to reliably identify key sleep stages (N1, N3, REM).
- **Key Takeaway**: Evaluating models via Leave-One-Patient-Out (LOPO) cross-validation is essential for uncovering true subject generalization issues that K-fold random window splitting masks.
- **Recommended Action Plan**:
  1. **Class Re-weighting / Focal Loss**: Apply class-balanced loss weights or Focal Loss to equalize gradient contributions across stages.
  2. **Sequence / Temporal Modeling**: Pass sequences of consecutive epochs into an LSTM/GRU or Transformer to model sleep stage transition dynamics over time.
  3. **Subject-Wise Feature Standardisation**: Apply robust per-subject normalization to reduce inter-subject amplitude variation.
  4. **Multi-Scale Feature Extractor**: Utilize multi-kernel conv layers or spectrogram-based inputs to capture distinct EEG frequency bands.
